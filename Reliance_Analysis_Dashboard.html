1

Understand the Business

Forget complicated words for a moment. What does this company actually sell, to whom, and how does it make money on it?

You've probably already used Reliance's products

If you've ever recharged a Jio SIM card, bought vegetables at Reliance Fresh or clothes on Ajio, filled petrol at a Jio-bp pump, or used anything made of plastic or polyester — you've been Reliance's customer without necessarily realizing it. This one company actually runs four completely different everyday businesses side by side.

🛢️ The Refinery & Plastics Business (officially called Oil-to-Chemicals)

What they buy: crude oil — the thick black liquid pumped out of the ground in the Middle East and elsewhere.

What they do with it: at their giant Jamnagar refinery in Gujarat (the largest refinery complex in the world at a single location), they heat and separate crude oil into petrol, diesel, jet fuel, cooking gas (LPG), and raw chemical material.

What they sell: fuel through Jio-bp petrol pumps, and chemical raw material to other factories that turn it into the plastic bag, water bottle, or polyester shirt you use every day.

How they make money: buy crude oil at the global market price, then sell the fuel and chemicals for more, combined, than that crude cost. That gap is called a "refining margin" — and it moves up and down with global oil markets, entirely outside Reliance's control.

⛽ The Gas-Drilling Business (officially Oil & Gas / E&P)

What they do: instead of buying crude oil from someone else, this business drills its own wells — mainly under the sea off India's east coast, in a field called KG-D6 — and pulls out natural gas and oil directly from the ground.

Who buys it: fertiliser factories, power plants, and city gas-pipeline networks across India.

How they make money: the cost of drilling and extracting, versus the price they're allowed to sell the gas at (partly regulated by the Indian government). A very high profit margin per unit, but a much smaller business overall than the other three.

📶 The Phone & Internet Business (officially Digital Services / Jio)

What they built: mobile towers, undersea cables, and fibre-optic lines across the entire country — an enormous, one-time cost running into lakhs of crores of rupees.

What you pay for: your monthly mobile recharge, or your JioFiber home broadband bill.

How they make money: once the network already exists, serving one more customer costs very little extra — but that customer pays a fee every single month. This is why, rupee for rupee, the phone & internet business is Reliance's single most profitable one (you'll see this in real numbers in Step 4).

🛒 The Shops You Walk Into (officially Retail)

What it is: Reliance Fresh and Smart Bazaar (groceries), Reliance Trends and Ajio (clothes), Reliance Digital (electronics), and JioMart (online shopping) — over 20,000 stores across India.

How they make money: buy products in bulk, directly from manufacturers and farmers, at wholesale prices — then sell to you at a retail price. It's the exact same "buy low, sell high" idea as your neighbourhood shopkeeper, just done at a massive, nationwide scale.

Put simply: owning one Reliance share means owning a small slice of a petrol pump, a gas well, your phone network, and your local supermarket — all bundled into a single stock. Each of these four makes money in a totally different way, which is exactly why we look at each one separately before judging the whole company.

Why can't someone just start a competing business tomorrow?

  • Refinery & Plastics: building a refinery like Jamnagar costs billions of dollars and takes years to construct — very few companies in the world can even attempt this.
  • Gas Drilling: requires a government licence to drill in a specific area, plus expensive deep-sea drilling technology.
  • Phone & Internet: needs a government-auctioned spectrum licence (which itself costs tens of thousands of crores) plus years spent laying physical cable and towers nationwide.
  • Retail: needs a nationwide supply chain and warehousing network built over many years — hard for a new entrant to replicate quickly.

These barriers are what analysts mean when they talk about a company's "moat" — reasons a new competitor can't easily walk in and steal the business away.

Reflection: now that you know how each of the four businesses actually earns its money, which one do you think investors get most excited about for the future — and why?

The Three Financial Statements — From Scratch

Every public company publishes three documents, every quarter and every year. Before we read Reliance's real numbers, let's understand what each document actually is.

Start with an analogy from your own life

Income Statement
= Your monthly salary slip. Money earned (salary) minus money spent (rent, groceries, EMIs) = what's left over. It covers a period of time — a quarter or a year.
Balance Sheet
= A photo of your bank balance and loans, taken today. What you own (savings, house, car) vs. what you owe (home loan, credit card). One single date, not a period.
Cash Flow Statement
= Your bank statement. Every rupee that actually moved in or out, split into 3 taps: from your job (operating), from buying/selling big things like a car (investing), and from loans/EMIs (financing).
A company's "annual report" or "quarterly results" is simply these three documents, plus explanatory notes, filed with the stock exchange (NSE/BSE) and published on the company's investor-relations webpage. Anyone can download them for free — no special access needed. That's exactly where every table below comes from.

What the numbers say

  • RIL shares carry a face value of just ₹10 — a reminder that the ₹10 accounting par value has nothing to do with the ₹1,327 market price; the share simply trades at roughly 133x its face value.
  • A debt-to-equity ratio of 0.41x (officially disclosed by the company) means for every ₹1 of shareholder equity, RIL carries ₹0.41 of debt — conservative for a business this capital-hungry. You may see slightly different debt-to-equity figures on other websites; that's usually because each provider defines "debt" a little differently. Always check how a ratio is calculated before comparing it across sources.
  • The current ratio of 1.10x means short-term assets (cash, inventory, receivables) cover short-term debts (due within a year) comfortably, with a small buffer to spare.
  • Interest coverage stands at 5.55x — operating profit covers interest expense more than five times over, a comfortable cushion.
  • Net Debt to EBITDA is 0.60x — a common way analysts gauge how many years of operating profit it would take to clear all debt after using up all cash. Anything meaningfully below 1x is considered very comfortable.

Cash Flow, In Depth

Profit is an accounting opinion; cash is a fact. This step checks whether reported profit is "real."

Operating cash flow of ₹1,92,113 Cr running at roughly 2 times reported net profit (₹95,754 Cr) is a good sign — it means the profit shown in the Income Statement is well-backed by actual cash coming in, not just accounting adjustments.

But free cash flow — operating cash minus the ₹1,22,916 Cr spent on new plants, towers and network (capex) — leaves about ₹69,197 Cr "free," roughly 0.72x of net profit. That's not automatically bad — it tells you RIL is reinvesting heavily (5G, new energy, retail expansion) rather than banking the cash. The trade-off: less cash is "free" right now for large dividends or fast debt paydown.

Reflection: heavy capex today is a bet on tomorrow's earnings. The open question for any investor is whether that spending eventually converts into proportionately higher future profit — or just higher depreciation and debt.

Reading the ROE number honestly

An ROE of roughly 8.9% is on the lower side for a company of RIL's stature — it means the company generated about ₹8.90 of profit for every ₹100 of shareholder equity. One honest explanation: large amounts of capital have been deployed into Retail, Jio, and New Energy that haven't yet reached full earnings potential relative to the capital invested. Whether that capital eventually earns its keep is one of the central questions for anyone studying this stock.

A stock is never "cheap" or "expensive" in isolation — P/E and P/B only mean something when compared against the company's own history, and against similar businesses (peers). We've deliberately not done that peer comparison yet — that's a natural next step once you're comfortable reading Reliance on its own.

RIL paid ₹5.50/share for FY24-25 and its board recommended ₹6.00/share for FY25-26 — dividends are paid once a year. A sub-1% yield is low in absolute terms, but that's a deliberate pattern for a company still in a heavy capex/growth phase (see Step 6): profit is being redirected into building Jio's network, expanding retail stores, and the New Energy business, rather than distributed as cash.

General pattern worth remembering: a low dividend yield isn't automatically a bad sign. It often signals a growth-and-reinvestment strategy rather than a cash-cow, mature-business strategy. The two require different expectations from an investor.

Risks & Catalysts to Track

What could change this story — for better or worse — from here?

Watch Jio IPO (expected 2026)

  • Jio Platforms carries roughly ₹70,781 Cr of borrowings (as of Mar 2026) with restrictive loan conditions.
  • A disputed government demand of ~₹1,389 Cr (an extra telecom licence charge) is pending.
  • Reliance Retail is Jio's sole distributor for prepaid recharges — this related-company arrangement made up ~77% of Jio's revenue in FY26, a concentration worth understanding.
  • Making money from 5G at full scale remains an open question for the whole telecom industry, not just Jio.

Watch Retail IPO (expected 2027)

  • Reliance's retail arm could be valued near $200 billion at IPO, per market estimates.
  • Both planned IPOs raise a fair question: will existing RIL shareholders fully capture the value being unlocked, or will some of it stay "trapped" at the parent-company level (sometimes called a holding company discount)?

Structural risk Refinery Business Is Cyclical

The refinery & plastics business is exposed to global crude oil prices and refining margins — factors entirely outside the company's control, driven by global supply/demand and geopolitics.

Operational Execution Risk

Large, ongoing spending programmes (5G, New Energy/green hydrogen, retail expansion) carry execution risk — and isolated incidents like a past multi-hour 5G/JioAirFiber outage are reminders that scale brings operational complexity.

Putting It Together

No single number tells the whole story. Here's how the nine steps above connect.

  • Business (Step 1): four very different everyday businesses bundled into one stock — a refinery, a gas well, a phone network, and a supermarket chain.
  • Reading the statements (Step 2): profit flows from the Income Statement into the Cash Flow Statement, and cash flows from there into the Balance Sheet — three views of the same company.
  • Ownership (Step 3): promoters just crossed 50%, a confidence signal — but FII selling is worth monitoring over more quarters.
  • Growth (Step 4): revenue and record profit are growing, powered mainly by the phone & internet business and the shops.
  • Balance sheet (Step 5): leverage is conservative (0.41x debt-to-equity) with comfortable interest cover, even as absolute debt rises to fund growth.
  • Cash quality (Step 6): profit is well-backed by cash, but heavy spending on new projects means free cash is thin right now.
  • Valuation (Step 7): trades at roughly 2x book value with an ROE that looks modest today — the bet is that current spending converts into higher future ROE.
  • Shareholder returns (Step 8): low dividend yield, consistent with a reinvestment-first strategy.
  • Risks (Step 9): two major IPOs on the horizon (Jio, Retail) are the biggest swing factors — potential value-unlocking events that also carry execution and structural risk.
This is not a verdict — it's a map. The real skill in analysis is holding all ten steps in your head at once, and updating your view every quarter as new numbers arrive.
